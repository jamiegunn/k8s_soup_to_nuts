## Readability contract (applies to every page in this section)

Target reader: a delivery-team developer who can deploy their app (Helm/CI exists) and has heard "the cluster can autoscale" but has never written an HPA and doesn't know Kubernetes internals.

- Every page's first section explains its topic in plain language before any YAML. The overview explains autoscaling itself from zero ("a controller watches a number about your app; when the number crosses a line, it adds or removes copies of your pod").
- Every Kubernetes/tooling term gets a one-sentence plain definition on first use per page (HPA, ScaledObject, scrape, replica, requests…), then a link to the site's deep page for the mechanics. Links supplement, never substitute, the sentence.
- No unexplained jargon, no "obviously/simply". Formulas get a worked example with real numbers immediately after.
- Each reference-architecture page opens with "What you'll have at the end" in plain words (mirrors the labs' device).
- YAML samples are complete enough to adapt (not fragments floating without context), with every knob explained in an inline comment in plain language.
- **Complete-commands contract:** every kubectl/helm command is shown in full — real resource names from the cast, explicit `-n <namespace>`, no elided flags, no `...`, no assumed aliases or current-context tricks. Each command gets: one plain sentence of *what it does and why you're running it* before it, and a `console` block of representative output after it, with the line(s) to look at called out. Where a command reads state (`kubectl get hpa payments-api -n payments`, `kubectl describe hpa`, `kubectl top pods`), show how to *read* the output column by column the first time it appears in the section.
- **The why, always:** no knob, threshold, or recommendation appears without its reason attached — "set X to Y" is always "set X to Y *because* Z". If the reason is a formula, the formula is shown; if it's a failure story, the story is told in one sentence.
- **Tradeoffs are stated, not implied:** every meaningful choice (signal, mechanism, threshold, min/max, stabilization window, scale-to-zero, Prometheus vs Dynatrace path…) carries an explicit "what you gain / what you pay" — as a two-column mini-table for big decisions or a single "The trade:" sentence for small ones (matches the site's existing `:::note[...the honest trade]` idiom). No recommendation without its cost.
- **Define → observe → decide, for every measurement term:** statistical and metric vocabulary (p95/percentiles, utilization, rate, average-vs-percentile, saturation…) is never just defined. Each gets the full loop on first load-bearing use: a plain-language definition ("p95 = the time 95 of 100 requests beat — the experience of your unluckiest 1-in-20 user"), **how to observe it** (the exact PromQL / Grafana panel / Dynatrace view, complete per the commands contract, plus how to read what comes back), and **how to decide from it** (an "if you see X → it means Y → do Z" rule or mini-table). A number the reader can't fetch or act on is trivia, not guidance.

## Good citizenship on requests (runs through the whole section)

The cluster is fixed-size and shared; **requests are reservations, not usage** — capacity you claim is capacity your neighbors can't schedule into, whether you use it or not. This is the section's citizenship contract, stated in overview and enforced everywhere:

- Requests are **measured, not padded**: the "request what you measured + a justified margin" rule (→ tuning/sizing-walkthrough, tuning/requests-limits-knobs); padding requests "to be safe" silently shrinks everyone else's headroom and inflates every HPA-percentage calculation built on them.
- The autoscaling multiplier: an HPA multiplies whatever your requests are — honest requests × more replicas = fair claim; padded requests × more replicas = hoarding at scale. maxReplicas × requests is *the* number the platform ledger tracks (→ capacity-and-governance invariant).
- minReplicas honesty: idle replicas held "just in case" are reservations too (→ operations/cost-and-rightsizing).
- The proof commands: complete kubectl/PromQL to compare requested vs actually-used (`kubectl top` vs requests; the promql-for-resources consumed-vs-requested queries) appear in prerequisites (item 1), quick-start (the watch-it-for-a-week queries), and capacity-and-governance (the quarterly true-up).
- Recurring `:::tip[Good citizen]` aside device wherever a knob could be set selfishly (requests, minReplicas, maxReplicas, PriorityClass, scale-to-zero refusal).

## The SLO thread (runs through the whole section)

Every scaling number in this section must be traceable to a promise about users — or honestly labeled as a provisional system-level proxy. The device is the **translation ladder**, taught once on its own page (`slos-for-scaling.md`) and then *applied* everywhere:

```
user behavior → user-visible symptom → SLI (the measurement) → SLO (the promise) → scaling signal + threshold
```

- The recurring cast gets a canonical SLO table on the SLO page (e.g. `payments-api`: 99.9% of requests < 800 ms over 28 days; `dispatch-worker`: 99% of dispatch messages processed within 5 minutes; `notify-worker`: notifications within 15 minutes; `catalog-web`: 99% < 1 s) — and those *same numbers* reappear as the derivation source on the reference-architecture pages, so readers watch an SLO become an HPA target, a queueLength, and a grace period.
- **The fallback ladder** (explicit, non-judgmental): (a) best — a user-impact SLO agreed with the product owner; (b) good — a proxy SLO derived from observed behavior ("current p95 + safety margin, labeled PROVISIONAL"); (c) floor — a pure system objective ("CPU < 70%", "queue drains by 06:00") written down *as* an objective with an explicit TODO to upgrade it. Every artifact (golden values `# derivation:` comment, review checklist) accepts all three levels but requires the level to be stated — that requirement is what trains teams up the ladder.
- Consumers think in **freshness**, not latency: "processed within N minutes" is the SLO shape that directly produces the KEDA queueLength math.

### 4. prerequisites.md — Before You Autoscale (no assumptions)
Framing: "An HPA pointed at an unprepared app doesn't fail loudly — it makes things worse quietly. Here is everything that must be true first, with a command to prove each one." Structured as a numbered checklist; **every item = why it matters (plain words) + a `bash` check + a `console` "good" output + what to do / who to ask if it fails**:
1. Your pods declare CPU/memory **requests** (define requests in one sentence: "the slice of a node reserved for your pod — reserved whether you use it or not") — complete command with real names (`kubectl get deployment payments-api -n payments -o jsonpath='{.spec.template.spec.containers[0].resources}'` + console output + how to read it); without requests, percentage-based scaling is mathematically undefined (→ tuning/requests-limits-knobs). 2. The cluster can measure CPU at all: `kubectl top pods` works (metrics-server, defined) — if `error: Metrics API not available` → PLATFORM row.
3. Readiness and liveness probes exist and are honest (defined in one sentence each) — a scaled-out pod that reports ready before it can serve makes scaling *hurt* (→ tuning/health-check-design).
4. Your app survives being stopped: graceful shutdown verified (preStop/grace, → workloads/graceful-shutdown) — scale-IN kills pods on purpose; autoscaling multiplies whatever your shutdown behavior is.
5. For consumers: message handling is **idempotent** (defined) — scale-in requeues in-flight messages; at-least-once delivery is a precondition (→ messaging-consumers).
6. Someone scrapes your metrics if you'll scale on anything beyond CPU: `/actuator/prometheus` reachable + ServiceMonitor exists (→ getting-the-metrics, observability/metrics).
7. You are *allowed* to create the objects: RBAC check (`kubectl auth can-i create horizontalpodautoscalers`), namespace ResourceQuota headroom check, and — for queue scaling — is KEDA even installed (`kubectl get crd scaledobjects.keda.sh`)? Each failure = a named ask to the platform team (→ operations/working-with-platform-team).
8. You can generate load in a pre-prod environment (→ Lab 8's fortio pattern; workloads/autoscaling §load-test) — an untested HPA is a hypothesis.
9. `replicas:` is out of your Helm chart when the HPA is on (the Helm-vs-HPA tug-of-war, plain-language explanation + the `{{- if not .Values.autoscaling.enabled }}` idiom) — foreshadows Lab 10.
→ Summary table: item / check command / owner if broken (YOU vs PLATFORM vs DBA/broker admin). → `:::tip` print-this-checklist framing (it reappears as the review gate on capacity-and-governance), copyable-markdown formatted per the navigation layer. ### 5. classify-your-app.md — Rationalize the App and the Chart (NEW — the intake exercise)
Framing: "Two copies of some apps is twice the capacity. Two copies of others is a bug. Before any autoscaler touches your service, answer what it *is* — then check whether your chart can even express the answer." Three parts, each producing a filled-in artifact:

**Part 1 — Can N copies safely coexist? (the safety audit).** The Spring-specific hazards, each with plain-language why, a concrete *how to check* (the exact thing to grep for in code/config — complete commands), and the remediation or the honest verdict "not horizontally scalable yet":
- `@Scheduled`/Quartz jobs — N replicas = N firings (the classic: the nightly report emailed four times). Check: `grep -rn "@Scheduled\|@EnableScheduling" src/`; fix: ShedLock / Quartz clustered mode / extract to a CronJob (→ workloads/jobs-and-cronjobs). The trade of each fix stated.
- In-memory state — HTTP sessions, local caches, in-JVM rate limiters: scale-out fragments them, scale-in deletes them. Check: session config + `@Cacheable` without a shared backend; fix → external Redis/Valkey (→ web-worker-and-caches).
- Startup migrations — Flyway/Liquibase on N simultaneous cold starts: lock contention or worse. Check: migration-on-startup config; fix: migrations as a pre-deploy Job/CI step, or verify the locking story and startupProbe headroom.
- Exclusive consumers & ordering — IBM MQ exclusive open, RabbitMQ single-active-consumer, strict message ordering: parallelism is capped at 1 (or per-group). Check: queue/listener config; verdict: KEDA maxReplicaCount honors it or the design changes (→ messaging-consumers).
- Local filesystem writes, websockets/long-lived connections (→ networking/long-lived-connections), anything that elects a leader. 
Output: a pass/fail row per hazard. `:::danger[Fail = stop]` — an HPA on an unsafe app converts load spikes into data bugs; fix first, scale second. (VPA-style vertical sizing remains available meanwhile → tuning pages.)

**Part 2 — Which archetype is it? (the categorization).** Four sorting questions, plain words: How does work arrive (requests / queue messages / the clock)? What does it spend time on when busy (CPU / waiting on Oracle-MQ / a downstream)? What state does it hold (none / cache / sessions)? What SLO shape fits (latency / freshness / deadline)? → **Mermaid: sorting flowchart** ending in the three reference-architecture pages (+ "batch/cron → CronJob+ScaledJob, out of this section's scope" exit + "mixed → split the chart" → web-worker-and-caches). → **The classification card** (the page's artifact, a fill-in template): archetype / safety findings / dependency inventory with each ceiling and its owner (Oracle sessions-DBA, MQ handles-MQ admin, Redis maxclients…) / SLO shape / chart-audit grade (Part 3) — this card is what the team carries into slos-for-scaling and eventually attaches to the governance submission.

**Part 3 — Is the chart ready? (the Helm audit).** Complete-commands walkthrough of auditing a chart you may not have written: `helm get values orders -n payments` (what's actually deployed), `helm template charts/orders-api | grep -A3 replicas` (what the chart renders — each command with why + how to read output). ### 7. load-profile.md — Know Your Traffic: Steady State, Low State, Peak (NEW)
Framing: "You can't pick a floor and a ceiling for a load you've never measured. - **Steady state** — typical busy-hours load. - **Low state** — the trough (overnight, weekends). Observe: `quantile_over_time(0.05, ...)`. Decide: minReplicas = what the trough needs (with the HA floor ≥ 2 → workloads/high-availability); `:::tip[Good citizen]` — holding steady-state replicas through the trough is reserved capacity nobody else can use; for consumers, low state ≈ 0 is the scale-to-zero test.
- **Peak** — the *recurring* high (lunch spike, Monday open). - **Growth trend** — this quarter vs last. Observe: `avg_over_time` week-over-week comparison promql. Decide: the margin in the maxReplicas math + when to re-run this whole exercise.

### 8. signals-catalog.md — The Numbers That Matter
Framing: "The most common autoscaling failure is a working autoscaler pointed at the wrong number." Plain-language opener: a good scaling signal predicts an SLO breach early — it rises *before* users hurt and falls when they stop; a bad one is a symptom, a lie, or a ratchet. (The SLO comes first → slos-for-scaling; this page picks the number that protects it. Each catalog entry carries a one-line "SLO shape it protects" field — latency SLOs → threads/RPS/CPU; freshness SLOs → queue depth/lag.) → **The catalog** — one subsection per signal, each following the define→observe→decide template: what it means in plain words; where it comes from (source system) and the actual metric name; **observe** — the complete query to see it now + how to read the result; **decide** — the "if you see → it means → do" rule; scale on it / alert on it / rightsize on it; and the trap:
- **CPU utilization** (kubelet/metrics-server; `container_cpu_usage_seconds_total` in Prometheus) — the default; honest only for CPU-bound work; lies for apps that spend their time *waiting* on Oracle/MQ (which is most of this stack). Scale-on: yes, when measured CPU-bound.
- **Queue depth / oldest-message age** (broker-side: IBM MQ CURDEPTH via admin REST, RabbitMQ queue length via management API; KEDA polls these directly) — the correct signal for consumers; depth vs lag-time trade-off. Scale-on: yes (via KEDA).
- **Thread exhaustion** (Micrometer: `tomcat_threads_busy_threads` vs `tomcat_threads_config_max_threads`; executor queue size) — the leading indicator for blocking-I/O Spring apps: threads saturate long before CPU when the app waits on external systems. Scale-on: yes (custom metric). Plain-language sidebar on what a servlet thread pool is.
- **JVM heap vs pod memory delta** (`jvm_memory_used_bytes` vs `container_memory_working_set_bytes`) — what the gap is (metaspace, threads, direct buffers, native), what a *growing* gap means (native leak) vs a stable one (normal overhead). **Scale-on: NO — rightsizing and alerting signal only**; `:::danger` HPA-on-memory + JVM = ratchet to maxReplicas forever (→ java/jvm-kubernetes-coupling, tuning/jvm-memory-knobs).
- **RPS per pod** (Micrometer `http_server_requests_seconds_count`) — needs a measured per-pod capacity number (→ tuning/sizing-walkthrough). Scale-on: yes, with homework.
- **Latency p95** — `:::danger[Don't scale on p95]`: it's the symptom, saturates late, oscillates (cold pods post the worst latency → scaling *causes* the signal). Alert-on only.
- **Connection-pool pressure** (`hikaricp_connections_pending`) — tells you "pods *or* pool"; decide with threads. Alert-on; feeds ceiling math (→ rest-api-oracle).
- **GC pressure** (`jvm_gc_pause_seconds`) — alert/rightsize, not scale.
→ **The summary table** (the page's artifact): signal / plain meaning / metric name / source / scale-alert-rightsize / archetype it fits. → **Mermaid: decision flowchart** ("Is work pulled from a queue?" → queue depth via KEDA; "request/response?" → "measured CPU-bound?" → CPU HPA; else threads or RPS custom metric; "number only exists in Dynatrace?" → dynatrace-signals) — with `click` directives routing nodes to pages (verify the plugin passes them through; fallback: link legend under the diagram). → Mechanism table: HPA-on-CPU vs HPA-on-custom-metric vs KEDA — what each is (one plain sentence), metrics path, who installs (PLATFORM/YOU), scale-to-zero, what happens when the pipeline dies (KEDA fallback), and a gain/pay row per mechanism (e.g. CPU: zero setup / lies for waiting apps; custom: honest signal / you own a pipeline; KEDA: right consumer semantics + scale-to-zero / a new controller and CRDs in the mix). → Failure-mode table (memory → ratchet; latency → flap; CPU on waiting apps → scales too late; averaged signal hides one hot pod pinned by keep-alive → networking/long-lived-connections). → PromQL: signal-audit queries (per-pod CPU vs RPS vs busy-threads vs p95 side by side for one deployment — "eyeball which one moves first") + desired-replicas churn alert.
